Question 1¶

Mars is 2.3e8 km away from the Sun; its albedo is 0.15. Its only source of heat is solar radiation.

(a) Calculate the effective temperature of Mars.

Total radiation from the Sun's surface $E_S = 4\pi R_S^2\sigma T_S^4$

Radiation flux reaching at the Mar's TOA: $F_S = \frac{E_s}{4\pi d^2}$, d is the Sun-Mars distance

According to the radiative balance, $\sigma T_M^4 = \frac{Fs(1-A)}{4}$

The effective temperature on Mars is calculated: $T_M = 215.4 K$

(b) The temperature observed at the surface of Mars is 220 K. What do you conclude about the Martian atmosphere?

(1) Some greenhouse gases exit in the Martian atmoshpere and act to absorb the infared radiation. This is because the observed temperature is higher than the effective temperature.

(2) The atmohsphere on Mars is very thin. The difference between the observed temperature and effective temperature is small. According to the radiative balance, the downward radiation contributed by this thin atmoshpere can lead to a slightly increase in Martian surface temperature. Furthermore, the thin atmoshphere only contributes a small amount of incoming longwave radiaion to the surface. So the observed temperature is still close to the effective temperature numerically.

Question 3¶

Use the MODTRAN model to simulate the terrestrial infrared radiation spectrum at a location on Earth.

1. Use the default present-day conditions: CO2=400 ppm; CH4=1.7 ppm; tropospheric ozone=28 ppb; stratospheric ozone scale=1; water vapor scale=1; freon scale=1; temperature offset=0C; no clouds or rain; altitude 70 km; looking down; location: midlatitude winter

2.Using the Planck function, plot the blackbody radiation spectra at several different temperatures. Discuss why some of the features in the simulated spectra look like the Plank function at specific temperature

Answer: The atmoshpere has different absorptivity regarding to different wavelength. The monochromatic intensity of the radiation at any given wavelength indicates the temperature of the layer of the atmoshphere from which that radiation is emitted (Wallace & Hobbs, 2006). The outmost simulated spectra actually reflects the Earth's emitting radiation at a local surface temperature. At a certain range of wavelength associated with a greenhous gas (GHG) where the spectra looks like the Plank function, it reflects the monochromatic intensity of the upward radiation after absorbing by the GHG within a layer of atmoshpere. The temperature of the fitted curve of the Plank function reveals the temperature of that layer of atmoshpere.

# Code is obtained from the public sources of ESE315
def blackbody_lam(lam, T):
    """ Blackbody as a function of wavelength (micrometer) and temperature (K).

    returns units of erg/s/cm^2/cm/Steradian = 10^(-7)W/cm^2/cm/Steradian
    Unit: 1 erg = 10^(-7)J
    """
    from scipy.constants import h,k,c
    lam = 1e-6 * lam # convert to metres
    return 2*h*c**2 / (lam**5 * (np.exp(h*c / (lam*k*T)) - 1))

When double the CO2 concentration to 800 ppm, the upward IR flux represents an apparent decreasing at the range of wavelength of around 13-16 microns. CO2 and other greenhouse gases (GHGs) act to absorb infared radiation, which associates with the negative peak in the spectra. This phenomenon is consistent with the fact that increasing the concentration of GHGs have a positive radiative forcing. In the current situation, the CO2 is much more abundant than methane in the atmosphere.Considering the absorption band of methan is almost not overlapped with other GHGs, while CO2 and water vapor overlap somewhere. When adding a small amount of methane, it will contribute more radiation to be absorbed. But increasing a relative small amount of CO2 will not bring too much change to the upward infared intensity.
